    <h3>What do we do if an employee is returning from overseas now that the Australian Government imposed self-isolation when returning from international travel? Do we have to pay them? </h3>
    <div>
      <p>
        In line with the directions issued by the <a href="https://www.health.gov.au/news/health-alerts/novel-coronavirus-2019-ncov-health-alert" target="_blank">Department of Health</a>, the employee must isolate themselves at for 14 days from the day they return from overseas. Employers should offer employees the ability to use any accrued leave, such as annual leave, long service leave or personal leave, depending on circumstance. Things to consider include whether or not the employee is ill, if they are fit to work from home, or if the employee would agree to take paid annual leave.
      </p>
    </div>

    <h3>What are the guidelines for self-isolation? </h3>
    <div>
      <p>
        The <a href="https://www.health.gov.au/news/health-alerts/novel-coronavirus-2019-ncov-health-alert" target="_blank">Department of Health</a> has issued directions for the circumstances in which persons must isolate themselves. At the time of writing the guidelines issued by the
        <a href="https://www.health.gov.au/news/health-alerts/novel-coronavirus-2019-ncov-health-alert" target="_blank">Department of Health</a> are as follows;
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li><em>All persons who have travelled overseas and arrived in Australia from midnight (AEST) Sunday, 15 March 2020, or have been in close contact with a confirmed case of coronavirus (COVID-19) are required to self-isolate for 14 days.</em></li>       
      </ul>
      <p>CCIWA recommends reviewing the <a href="https://www.health.gov.au/news/health-alerts/novel-coronavirus-2019-ncov-health-alert" target="_blank">Department of Health's</a> guidelines on a regular basis, as the guidelines are developing and have already been updated several times.</p>
    </div>

    <h3>Do we have to pay casual employees if that are required to self-isolate? </h3>
    <div>
      <p>
        Casual employees do not have guaranteed hours of work and are not entitled to payment for cancellation of shifts unless there is an award, enterprise agreement, contract of employment or other industrial instrument stating they are entitled to payment of any kind (such as for a shift cancellation). Casual employees are not entitled to annual leave or personal leave where they become unfit for work. This includes where a casual employee contracts COVID-19.
      </p>
    </div>

    <h3>Can we stand down employees if we have a stoppage of work due to supply shortages?</h3>
    <div>
      <p>
        Employers should refer to their contracts of employment and any enterprise agreements first to ensure they do not contain any provisions regarding a stand down. Those provisions will take precedence. In the absence of any such provisions, if the business experiences a stoppage of work for which they cannot reasonably be held responsible, and the employees cannot usefully be employed otherwise, there may be the ability to stand the employees down without pay. Employers should make every effort to find alternatives and mitigate the adverse effects of a stand down event, such as offering any accrued leave entitlements to the employee.
      </p>
    </div>

    <h3>Do we need to make an employee redundant where we have no work?</h3>
    <div>
      <p>
        If a business experiences a downturn to the degree that they no longer require a job to be done by anyone, then redundancy may need to be considered. We recommend considering alternatives before reaching that decision, such as offering temporary alternative working arrangements, annual leave, accrued time off in lieu of overtime or leave without pay to employees that are agreeable. Any redundancy process should be accompanied by appropriate consultation where required.
      </p>
    </div>

    <h3>Do we have to shut down the business if an employee tests positive for COVID-19? </h3>
    <div>
      <p>
        Whole businesses do not need to be shut down because one employee tests positive for COVID-19. We recommend following the <a href="https://www.health.gov.au/news/health-alerts/novel-coronavirus-2019-ncov-health-alert" target="_blank">Department of Health's</a> guidelines which include self-isolation in the event of coming into close contact with a person that is a confirmed case of COVID-19. In a circumstance where employees can be isolated and still perform work, employees may be able to work from home.
      </p>
    </div>

    <h3>If an employee presents to work with flu- like symptoms, what should we do?</h3>
    <div>
      <p>
        Employees should be encouraged to stay away from the workplace in the event that they are ill in any case. If an employee appears unwell, presents for work and resists a suggestion to seek medical advice and go home to rest, given the circumstances, the employer may be able to direct the employee to attend a medical appointment, at the cost of the employer, to ensure the employee is fit to perform work. If the employee is certified as being unfit for work, the employee should be placed on personal leave.
      </p>
    </div>

    <h3>Can we require employees to provide medical clearance before returning to work after a period of leave? </h3>
    <div>
      <p>
        Employers may be able to make such a direction if they have a policy in place that directs employees to provide fit for work clearances when travelling to countries their policy outlines as being high risk. This is on the basis that the policy is reasonable and based on a reliable source such as the Australian Government’s
        <a href="https://smartraveller.govcms.gov.au/" target="_blank">Smartraveller website</a>.
      </p>
    </div>
