<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
<a href='https://www.coronavirusus.info/2020/03/incubation-period.html'>Incubation Period</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-8463605041818738955' itemprop='description articleBody'>
The overwhelming majority of people (97.5%) studied, who were infected with SARS-CoV-2, presented symptoms within <b>11.5 days</b> of exposure with a median  of <b>5.1 days</b> until onset of symptoms (Lauer et al. 2020). By these estimates they conservatively estimated that around 100 of every 10,000 will develop symptoms after a 14 day quarantine (Lauer et al. 2020). The 181 cases they looked at were taken from newspaper sources outside of Wuhan but inside China and may reflect a bias toward outliers given the attention grabbing nature of the media (Lauer et al. 2020).<br />
<br />
Lauer SA, Grantz KH, Bi Q, et al. The Incubation Period of Coronavirus Disease 2019 (COVID-19) From Publicly Reported Confirmed Cases: Estimation and Application. Ann Intern Med. 2020; [Epub ahead of print 10 March 2020]. doi: <a href="https://doi.org/10.7326/M20-0504">https://doi.org/10.7326/M20-0504</a><br />
<br />
The mean incubation period of 55 patients, with laboratory confirmed COVID-19, was <b>8.42 days</b>. While the mean duration for a positive test result was <b>9.71 days</b> (Yang et al 2020). When comparing patients with and without pneumonia patients, those with pneumonia tested positive for a longer duration which was on average 10.45 days as compared to 9.24 days (Yang et al 2020). From onset of symptoms until the patient was discharged was 17.76 days but was as long as 24 days for those patients that develop pneumonia (Yang et al 2020). Earlier incubation periods presented around <b>5.2 days</b> may have been hampered by studying mostly patients who presented with severe pneumonia inside the outbreak zone early on in Wuhan,China and may represent cases with exposure to higher viral loads in the areas most affected such as hospitals, clinics, etc and difficulty tracking independent cases (Yang et al 2020).<br />
<br />
Yang, Penghui, et al. (2020). Epidemiological and clinical features of COVID-19 patients with and without pneumonia in Beijing, China." medRxiv.&nbsp;doi:<a href="https://doi.org/10.1101/2020.02.28.20028068">https://doi.org/10.1101/2020.02.28.20028068</a>

<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
<a href='https://www.coronavirusus.info/2020/03/virological-assessment.html'>Transmission: Mild or Typical Symptoms</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-4331455164996254600' itemprop='description articleBody'>
Samples collected from clinically mild cases of COVID-19, showed that SARS-Cov-2 is able to replicate in the tissue of the upper respiratory tract (URT), despite the minimal expression of ACE2 receptor (Woelfel et. al 2020). The presence of a polybasic furin-type cleavage site situated at the S1-S2 junction of the spike (S) protein of SARS-CoV-2, which SARS-CoV lacks, is one potential explanation for infection this area of the body by SARS-CoV-2 while SARS-Cov cannot effectively (Wolfel et. al 2020). <b>The extended tropism of SARS-CoV-2, in the URT, could be the reason for it's more efficient transmission, when compared to SARS-CoV, while patients are still exhibiting mild or typical symptoms</b> (Woelfel et. al 2020). Simple throat or nasopharyngeal swabs could be used to diagnose patients exhibiting mild and typical upper respiratory (UR) tract symptoms of COVID-19, in the early stages of infeciton (Woelfel et al. 2020).&nbsp; This is in contrast to SARS for which it showed little effectiveness (Woelfel et. al 2020). Because the viral load for SARS-CoV-2 in the URT peak at five days, any increase in viral load after seven days could be used as an indicator for aggravated&nbsp;symptoms (Woelfel et al. 2020). Additional evidence of a patient that had genetically distinct virus in her lungs and in her throat was presented (Woelfel et al. 2020).<br />
<br />
Woelfel, Ramon et al. (2020). Clinical presentation and virological assessment of hospitalized cases of coronavirus disease 2019. MedRxiv doi:<a href="https://doi.org/10.1101/2020.03.05.20030502">https://doi.org/10.1101/2020.03.05.20030502</a><br />
<br />
Put simply: Unlike SARS, the virus which causes COVID-19 are able to infect parts of your upper airway which seems to make it easier to spread. This is because your bodies cells are making copies of the virus in a part of your body (nose and throat) which can more readily make their way out into the environment around you.
